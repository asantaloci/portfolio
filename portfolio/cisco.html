INTRO Consolidating internal distributor tools and reimagining Cisco's high visibility workforce transition experience.

Duration: 1 year

Year: May 2020 - May 2021


Objective

Since Cisco relies on distributors to buy and sell their products to customers, it is important that the experience Cisco's distributors and the employees navigating the same interfaces when recording point of sales, location information, and more are easy to use. Unfortunately, this was not the case when I entered the internship. There was not a streamlined approach for users to take for any of the 4-5 applications they normally used, and it was even hard to keep track of where all the applications were in the first place (they were all notified by email, and none of the applications were in a common central place). So, I decided to take a crack at consolidating the applications, and making the experience easier for all stakeholders involved, without losing valuable information and processes.

Another Opportunity

Along the way of my process, I was given another task on a different team to create a full portfolio of applications for the transition out of Cisco's current HR model. This meant splitting my worktime, and since this new team/project was meant to be my new priority, I had to be able to complete what I set to do with my initial Distribution project, as well as begin a new workload of web development and wireframing for the HR reconstruction.

Solution

Thus, my solution for my summer internship was to create a consolidated page for all the distribution apps to be hosted on, and to complete the urgent portfolio of HR tools with my different teams. I was the only design/UX tech intern within all of my teams, so this would also be a challenge, as people were reliant on me to know what was best in a UX standpoint.

*Disclaimer* Most of the work I did with Cisco is confidential, so I will be describing some interfaces vaguely/showcasing problem areas the best I can!

Skills Needed:

Angular, HTML, CSS, JavaScript, Communication Skills, UX/UI Design and Presentation skills, and Wireframing/Prototyping.

Pt 1: Distributor Home Screen

This section will briefly go over the Distributor App consolidation page.

Initial Status

Before I entered my internship with Cisco, there was no sort of consolidation of these 4-5 different apps. This meant that these applications were only viewed through email links, which is not very accessible. This is why I was tasked to create a new interface for all of these apps to be presented, and have the apps be dynamic based on the type of role each user had.

Draft Interface

Fixes

There were a few things that I wanted to change and add to this current draft:

- There was no need for a hero image, since it would distract from the main purpose of the page: the accessibility of the applications

- The notifications and release events sections of the page had the same information, meaning that it was mainly used for filler material, so I wanted to remove at least one of those sections or reorganize how notifications would be used

- There was no area for the user's metrics or alerts for the different applications, which could be useful so each user could have an idea of what tasks they need to get done

- The interface was not that outdated, but I still wanted to make some UI changes to make it more in sync with the rest of the applications

My Thoughts

I think my new design is cleaner, and eliminates extra scrolling, and simplifies what needs to be present on the homepage. The hierarchy of the application makes sense, with the applications accessible as soon as you enter the page, as well as metrics to help users figure out what needs to be done.

Having a new alerts button at the top will provide a more detailed idea of what things need to be done as well, and can redirect the user to different apps based on the alert message. As for the news section, since it does not take priority, having the button on the top right side of the screen allows it to not take up real estate, unless needed.

Overall I think I made an effective homepage to house all of the different distributor applications, and give them visible metrics on their personal tasks that wasn't available before.

Pt 2: Specific Distributor Apps

This section will cover an analysis of one app in particular that required the most change, as well as my plan for consolidating all Distributor apps.

Current App Observations

My main observations on the current interface of one of the main distributor apps I focused on:

- There is very outdated styling/sizing throughout all applications (not up to date with the Cisco UI Kit, since it was last updated in early 2000s)

- The navigation itself was confusing, especially when there were two nav menus on the same screen

- Important data was presented in a cluttered table format, making it harder for users to navigate the information efficiently

- There were little to no instructions on the pages, making it difficult for new users to understand where to go unless taught step by step

*A lo-fi version of the application is pictured below*



Filter Page Observations

When you click into any of the dropdown child pages from the main navigation, most if not all of these pages include a filter, an excel table with all transaction information, and some sort of action to take.

Since all of these pages were practically the same, I decided to consolidate all of these pages into one solid page per topic, lessening the amount of navigating action that the user would have to do, and eliminating the repetition. I also wanted to update the look of the filter so it wouldn't take up almost the entire screen, since users of the app would only fill out 1-2 of these filter specifications. The excel table would also be changed because of the intense amount of horizontal scrolling, and I knew there would be a better way to present and take action on that information. *A lo-fi version of the application is pictured below*



My Fixes

Along with the changes I mentioned above, in my version of one of the consolidated pages, I have the table of information with only relevant data, without the need for horizontal scrolling. I also added an option to sort based on all, error (transactions that need to be fixed), and expired. Also in the table, there is an option to apply more filters if the user selects the "filter" button, which will expand a small section that has different filter specifications.

In terms of actions, for the specific example I have pictured, "Extend Expiration" and "New Submission" were the only subpages that had a different page with distinguished actions, so I decided to have them be their own separate buttons that would create a popup once clicked.

Conclusion and Next Steps

This was my very first internship in a large tech company, and overall it was very impactful to my experience and my understanding of how UX works in certain environments. I definitely did not have as much time to go into the research and interview aspects of UX, and I was expected to crank out wireframes over very short amounts of time, sometimes even less a night. However, I realized that due to the limited timeframe of the applications we were making in order to redesign all of HR, I understood that this was an uncommon practice at Cisco. Nonetheless, I learned to make quick decisions and create wireframes with purpose and functionality in mind, rather than aesthetics. I was also the only UX/design employee on my team, which meant that I had to reach out to others outside of my function in order to get a second opinion (but this was harder to do for the highly confidential apps that I worked on later). In the future, I'd like to work on more projects that allow me to experience the full process of how interfaces and experiences are made, possibly with a UX partner or group to bounce ideas off of, but I still think that this was a very valuable experience and gave me a look into the adult tech world.
